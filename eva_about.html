PRAY CRISTANTO / GAME DESIGNER / LAMPUNG, INDONESIA / EST. 2019

LOG-001
THE BEGINNING

I didn't get into game design because I wanted a career. I got into it because a game once made me cry — and I needed to understand how that was possible. How a sequence of rules and choices could reach through a screen and make a person feel something that lingered for days.

Growing up in Lampung, I spent more time inside games than outside them. Not to escape — to understand. Why does this feel tense? Why did that choice matter? Why do I feel guilty about a decision I made for a fictional character? I became obsessed with the invisible machinery underneath — the systems that produce feeling.

I'm not interested in making games that are fun. I'm interested in making games that mean something. Fun is a side effect of a system that works.

Pray Cristanto / Personal Design Philosophy

In 2019, I got my first chance to work inside a studio — as an intern programmer at Eternal Dream. Within a month, I had published a full mobile game on the Play Store. It wasn't polished. But it was real, it was mine, and it taught me that shipping something imperfect beats designing something perfect that never exists.

LOG-002
FINDING THE WORK

Programming taught me how games are built. Community management taught me how players actually behave — which is almost never how designers predict. Both were necessary. Neither was where I belonged.

Game design is the intersection of both: you need to think in systems and think like a player simultaneously. When I moved into the design role in 2021, it felt like the first time I was using all the parts of my brain at once.

The first major project was The Sun Shines Over Us — a visual novel about mental health, bullying, and recovery. Heavy subject matter. We had 6 possible endings. My job was to make every single one of them feel reachable and earned, through 47 choice nodes that I mapped, weighted, and verified across 30+ full playthroughs.

It reached 500,000 downloads. Players wrote to us saying it helped them talk to someone they'd been avoiding for years. That's not something you put on a resume. It's something you carry.

LOG-003
WHAT I ACTUALLY DO

Most people think game design is about coming up with cool ideas. That's maybe 10% of it. The other 90% is measurement.

When a player says "this feels unfair," there's a number somewhere that's wrong. When they say "this choice didn't matter," there's a weight that needs adjusting. When they say "I couldn't stop playing," there's a loop interval that accidentally hit the right frequency. My job is to find those numbers, understand why they produce the feeling they produce, and make them deliberate.

I design in Google Sheets before I design in Unity. I run simulations before I run tests. I ask "what should the player feel here?" before I ask "what mechanic goes here?" The feeling comes first. The system that produces it comes second.

The best mechanic is one the player never notices — because it's doing exactly what they expected, quietly, every time.
